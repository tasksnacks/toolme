Understand the three loan types

Amortized loan – fixed payments

This is the “normal” loan most people know. You make a regular payment each month (or week, etc.). Every payment includes some interest and some repayment of the amount you borrowed. Over time, the loan slowly shrinks until it is fully paid off.

Common examples:

  • Home loans (mortgages)
  • Car loans
  • Personal loans with monthly payments

In the calculator, choose “Amortized loan” if you want to see how big your regular payment is and how much total interest you will pay.

Deferred payment loan – one big payment at the end

With this type of loan you don’t make regular payments. Instead, interest builds up over the life of the loan and you pay everything in one lump sum at the end.

You might see this in:

  • Some short-term business loans
  • “Balloon” loans where most of the amount is due at the end

In the calculator, choose “Deferred payment loan” to see how large that final payment will be and how much interest builds up.

Bond – amount you get today vs. amount you pay back later

A bond is a type of loan usually used by governments or companies. You agree to pay back a fixed amount in the future, and in return you receive some money today (usually a smaller amount).

This calculator helps you:

  • Work out how much you receive today for a future payoff
  • See how much of the difference is effectively interest

In the calculator, choose “Bond” and enter the amount that must be paid back at maturity as the Predetermined Due Amount.

Loan basics in plain language

Interest rate

The interest rate is the “price” of borrowing money. A higher rate means you pay more over time. Even small differences in the rate can add up to a lot of money on long loans.

Compounding

Compounding means interest is added to your balance, and then new interest is charged on that higher amount. The more often it compounds (daily vs. yearly), the more you will pay in total.

Loan term

The term is how long the loan lasts. A longer term usually means smaller regular payments, but more interest paid overall. A shorter term means higher payments but less total interest.

Secured vs. unsecured

A secured loan is backed by something you own, like a house or a car. If you don’t pay, the lender can take that asset. Unsecured loans (like many personal loans or credit cards) have no collateral, so they often come with higher interest rates.
